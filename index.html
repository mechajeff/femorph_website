The Challenge of Creating Finite Element Meshes

Creating high quality finite element models for an individual part that is defined with design intent CAD geometry can be a time-consuming, labor-intensive process. The difficulty stems from the non-robust nature of CAD-based meshing. Even small changes in geometry can cause meshing algorithms to fail, requiring engineers to spend significant time repairing and remeshing models.

Opportunities with FEMORPH

Through design, manufacturing, and sustainment, FEMORPH addresses major challenges in engineering analysis across the entire product lifecycle.

1. Material Review Board (MRB)

Handling non-conforming parts often demands new meshes and extensive rework. FEMORPH creates models of damaged or out-of-spec parts in minutes, cutting cost and engineering effort.

2. Physics-Based Quality Control

Traditional tolerance-based assessments are giving way to Digital Twin–driven evaluations. FEMORPH allows part-specific geometry variations to be analyzed efficiently and consistently.

3. Repair Design & Blending

Subtractive repair design typically requires repeated remeshing of modified geometries. FEMORPH’s morphing technology applies geometry repairs directly to existing meshes.

4. Design Optimization

Iterative meshing during design studies slows down optimization and increases cost. FEMORPH enables rapid morphing of baseline meshes, accelerating design iteration cycles.

5. AI & Machine Learning Training Data

Generating large, consistent datasets for AI models requires hundreds of geometric variants. FEMORPH produces those efficiently, enabling high-fidelity, physics-based learning.

Born at AFRL, Hardened at Pratt & Whitney, Deployed with Multiple Industry Customers

FEMORPH was conceived and developed at the Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) to address the challenge of modeling as-manufactured geometries with simulation-grade accuracy.

The technology has been industry-hardened in collaboration with Pratt & Whitney, where it has demonstrated robust performance across industrial-scale use cases in digital twin creation, mesh adaptation, and optimization workflows.

FEMORPH has since been further deployed to new industry partners who use it to speed the development of advanced engineering systems.

Integrated with F-22 Maintenance, Repair, Overhaul Program

FEMORPH is in operational use at Air Force Sustainment Center at Tinker Air Force Base. It has been integral to the repair and maintenance of the Air Forces most advanced fighter aircraft.

Basic FEMORPH Target Surface Process

1. Collect Geometry Data

Geometry data is collected using high-precision tools such as coordinate measuring machines (CMMs) and optical 3D scanners. These produce detailed point clouds or surface meshes for each component.

2. Import into FEMORPH

The measurement data is imported into FEMORPH, where it can be analyzed alongside the baseline simulation model.

3. Align and Compare

FEMORPH aligns the measured geometry and baseline model, producing a detailed deviation map that quantifies local shape differences.

4. Morph the Mesh

Robust mesh morphing transfers the measured geometric deviations to the simulation model, eliminating the need for time-consuming remeshing.

5. Save the script for automated morphing next data set

The steps performed in the GUI are implementing python function calls. These are recorded into a script for reuse.
