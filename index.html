Engineering Analysis Relies on Computational Meshes

Computer simulations of aero, thermal, and structural behavior of products is are relied on for advanced product development and are supported by the multi-billion dollar CAE industry. These simulations use compuational meshes to discretized 3D objects into nodes and elements that allow description of the object with large sets of equations that are solved to give physical behavior. These results are used in design, manufacturing, and sustainment of all advanced products.

Whether they are meshes for computational fluid dynamics (CFD), heat transfer, or structurlal models, they all share one thing in common...Generating these meshes can be hard.

The Challenge of Creating Computational Meshes

Creating high quality finite element models for an individual part that is defined with design intent CAD geometry can be a time-consuming, labor-intensive process.

The difficulty stems from the non-robust nature of CAD-based meshing. Even small changes in geometry can cause meshing algorithms to fail, requiring engineers to spend significant time repairing and remeshing models.

FEMORPH Addresses Meshing Challengs with Mesh Morphing

FEMORPH addresses mesh generation challenges by moving, rather than rebuilding computational meshes. It enables a particular approach, Target Surface morphing, that separates it from most competing commercial approaches. This morphing approach allows an existing computational model to be morphed to a wide range of measurement data or CAD geomries in a way that enables important engineering workflows.

In addition to target surface morphing, FEMORPH includes functionality that supports blend repair and Free Form morphing. This webpage gives further detail on these workflows and FEMORPH's possible use cases.

Born at AFRL, Hardened at Pratt & Whitney, Deployed with Multiple Industry Customers

FEMORPH was conceived and developed at the Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) to address the challenge of modeling as-manufactured geometries with simulation-grade accuracy.

The technology has been industry-hardened in collaboration with Pratt & Whitney, where it has demonstrated robust performance across industrial-scale use cases in digital twin creation, mesh adaptation, and optimization workflows.

FEMORPH has since been further deployed to new industry partners who use it to speed the development of advanced engineering systems.

The FEMORPH Target Surface Morphing Process

1. Collect Geometry Data

Geometry data is collected using high-precision tools such as coordinate measuring machines (CMMs) and optical 3D scanners. These produce detailed point clouds or surface meshes for each component.

2. Import into FEMORPH

The measurement data is imported into FEMORPH, where it can be analyzed alongside the baseline simulation model.

3. Align and Compare

FEMORPH aligns the measured geometry and baseline model, producing a detailed deviation map that quantifies local shape differences.

4. Morph the Mesh

Robust mesh morphing transfers the measured geometric deviations to the simulation model, eliminating the need for time-consuming remeshing.

5. Save the script for automated morphing next data set

The steps performed in the GUI are implementing python function calls. These are recorded into a script for reuse.

BLENDER: the Blend Repair Morphing Tool

FEMPORPH has capabilities beyond target surface morphing. It's Blender module streamlines the workflow to design blend repairs, a subtractive manufaturing approach that grinds and polishes damaged regions. These repairs are common in the rotating machinery industries and are valued for the low-cost return to service capability.

Blender contains a library of repair geometries, applies these to user-defined locations, and morphs the model to the intended repaired shape. The library includes both edge and and surface geometries. Repaired models can be based on the nominal models or, when available, the as-measured models that have been generated from FEMORPH's target surface capability.

Blender can be utiliezed through the GUI interface or through conveinient python scripting. This scripting allows for easy integration into automated workflows and allows optimization of repair designs to extract the most return on investment.

3D Optimization Workflows and Rapid Design Updates

FEMORPH is not limited to measurement data, it can morph meshes to CAD geometries in either IGES or STL formats. This allows the integration of FEMORPH and parametric CAD models within an optimization framework to produce the high performance parts that meet design constraints. FEMORPH ensures that the optimizer is not interupted by mesh generation errors. FEMORPH integrates with CAD geometries (IGES or STL) and parametric models to enable optimization without mesh generation failures.

While optimization workflows are one use of FEMORPH, it is also notable that in many casesanalysis groups get design updates that come from other teams involved with designing the system. In these cases, FEMORPH can morph the existing model to the new CAD definition to rapidly determine sutability of changes.

Integrated Multi-disciplinary Analysis Workflows

Improving the integrated design processes is necessary to reduce design time and produce products that optimize mission potential. One challenge faced in this process is the integration of aero, thermal, structures, and lifing groups workflows into an automated process. A reason for this challenge is that each group often uses different computational meshes and software in there operations that require their own expert and specialized users. This has contributed to a partially stovepiped, rather than truly integrated teams.

FEMORPH addresses this challenge by allowing different groups to interact with another's mesh using mesh morphing. For example, as a CFD group modifies their aerodynamic shape defined by "O" grids, "H" grids, or polyhedral elements, they can see the impact on heat transfer tetrahedral models and structural hexahedral models by using FEMORPH on the each source mesh

Experimental Validation

FEMORPH and the as-manufactured modeling process, including structured light scanning, has been validated on numerous case studies. Many of these studies have centered on predicting the mistuned behavior of integrally bladed rotors (IBRs). The modal dynamic response of these critical compoents is extremely sensitive to small variations in geometry, therefore making it at challenging test case for FEMORPH.

As part of 2025 validation efforts, an extensive study was performed the AFRL PBS Stage 5 Research Rotor. An iterative structured light measurement process was conducted to establish scan-to-scan measurement error less than 0.003 in. This measured surface was aligned with FEMORPH to an available PBS Stage 4 finite element model and morphed into an the as-manufactured PBS 5 model.

This models frequencies and mode shapes were compared to experimental vibration data collected on a traveling wave excitation system, with an array of speakers exciting each airfoil. A dense set of measurement points were collected with a Polytec laser vibrometer.

This dataset represents one of the highest fidelity measurements of rotor vibration response, and the figure on the left below shows a high frequency mistuned mode, showing archetypical mode localization. The figure on the right, shows that the as-manufactured model created with FEMORPH matches the prediction of this complex mode.

Validations such as this give FEMORPH customers the confidence to use the software on a wide range of failure critical parts.
