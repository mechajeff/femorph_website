Engineering Relies on Computational Meshes

Meshes enable accurate physics-based modeling of complex structural, heat transfer, and fluid flow behaviors that inform design, manufacturing, and sustainment decisions.

Meshing is a Engineering Bottleneck

Creating high quality meshes for individual part that is time-consuming, labor-intensive, and requires specialized expertise.

Difficulty arises from non-robust nature of CAD-based meshing. Even small changes in geometry can cause meshing algorithms to fail, requiring engineers to spend significant time repairing and remeshing models.

Traditional meshing limits engineers ability to look at design, manufacturing, and sustainement opportunities.

FEMORPH Resolves Meshing Challenges

FEMORPH addresses mesh generation challenges by transforming, rather than rebuilding computational meshes. Its distinguishing ability is Target Surface morphing

Target Surface Morphing updates an existing meshed model is transformed to a new 2D or 3D geometry defined by measurement data or CAD modeling.

FEMORPHs blend repair and aribitray shape morphing moduels make it a comprehensive workflow solution.

When combined FEMORPHs catalogue of other automated workflow features, it presents capabilities that have impacts throughout the product life cyle.

Born at AFRL, Hardened at Pratt & Whitney, Deployed with Multiple Industry Customers

FEMORPH was conceived and developed at the Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) to address the challenge of modeling as-manufactured geometries with simulation-grade accuracy.

The technology has been industry-hardened in collaboration with Pratt & Whitney, where it has demonstrated robust performance across industrial-scale use cases in digital twin creation, mesh adaptation, and optimization workflows.

FEMORPH has since been further deployed to new industry partners who use it to speed the development of advanced engineering systems.

Integrated with F-22 Maintenance, Repair, Overhaul Program

FEMORPH is in operational use at Air Force Sustainment Center at Tinker Air Force Base. It has been integral to the repair and maintenance of the Air Forces most advanced fighter aircraft.

The FEMORPH Target Surface Morphing Process

1. Collect Geometry Data

Geometry data is collected using high-precision tools such as coordinate measuring machines (CMMs) and optical 3D scanners. These produce detailed point clouds or surface meshes for each component.

2. Import into FEMORPH

The measurement data is imported into FEMORPH, where it can be analyzed alongside the baseline simulation model.

3. Align and Compare

FEMORPH aligns the measured geometry and baseline model, producing a detailed deviation map that quantifies local shape differences.

4. Morph the Mesh

Robust mesh morphing transfers the measured geometric deviations to the simulation model, eliminating the need for time-consuming remeshing.

5. Save the script for automated morphing next data set

The steps performed in the GUI are implementing python function calls. These are recorded into a script for reuse.

Explore free form design with FEMORPHs Arbitrary Morphing

Break Free from CAD Constraints, FEMORPH‚Äôs Arbitrary Morphing capability enables direct, physics-ready shape modification using control-point‚Äìdefined morphing volumes. By manipulating control points in space, engineers create smooth spatial deformation fields that morph existing meshes without requiring new CAD geometry. This allows rapid exploration of complex shape changes, ideal for early design studies, sensitivity analysis, and large-scale AI training data generation.

  • Control-Point Driven Morphing: Freely move 3D control points to define smooth spatial deformation fields.
  • Volume or Surface-Based Control: Use bounding volumes or surface-overlapping control points for refined local shaping.
  • CAD-Free Shape Variation: Generate complex geometry changes without rebuilding or parameterizing CAD models.
  • Physics-Ready Results: Preserve mesh quality and connectivity for immediate simulation.
  • Expanded Design Space: Explore non-intuitive and highly nonlinear shape variations.
  • AI Training Enablement: Rapidly generate large, diverse datasets without CAD workflow bottlenecks.

Arbitrary Morphing empowers engineers and data scientists to explore, analyze, and learn from geometry variations that would otherwise be impractical or impossible using traditional CAD-driven workflows.

Experimental Validation of FEMORPH and Part-specific Modeling

FEMORPH is validated through rigorous, physics-based experimental programs led by engineers and researchers, not just software developers. By integrating advanced metrology and structural dynamics testing, FEMORPH‚Äôs as-manufactured models are proven against some of the most demanding validation cases in structural mechanics.

  • Engineer-Led Validation: Developed and validated by researchers actively advancing metrology and experimental mechanics.
  • Integrated Validation Pipeline: Structured light scanning, model morphing, and experimental testing combined into a single workflow.
  • Improved Measurement Fidelity: Continuous refinement of structured light scanning accuracy for high-confidence geometry capture.
  • Advanced Vibration Testing: Specialized methods developed to measure mistuned dynamics in bladed rotors.
  • True Mistuning Physics: Captures blade-to-blade variability, modal coupling, and nonlinear sensitivity to micron-level geometry changes.
  • Proven Decision Confidence: Experimental correlation demonstrates FEMORPH models can be trusted for critical engineering decisions.
  • Fielded and Trusted: Validated technology relied upon by industry and the United States Air Force.

As part of 2025 validation efforts, an extensive study was performed the AFRL PBS Stage 5 Research Rotor. An iterative structured light measurement process was conducted to establish scan-to-scan measurement error less than 0.0003 in. This measured surface was aligned with FEMORPH to an available PBS Stage 4 finite element model and morphed into an the as-manufactured PBS 5 model.

This models frequencies and mode shapes were compared to experimental vibration data collected on a traveling wave excitation system, with an array of speakers exciting each airfoil. A dense set of measurement points were collected with a Polytec laser vibrometer.

This dataset represents one of the highest fidelity measurements of rotor vibration response, and the figure on the left below shows a high frequency mistuned mode, showing archetypical mode localization. The figure on the right, shows that the as-manufactured model created with FEMORPH matches the prediction of this complex mode.

Validations such as this give FEMORPH customers the confidence to use the software on a wide range of failure critical parts.
